Best for Fine Woodworking: Makita BO5041K

The Makita BO5041K is the sander for people who care about surface quality above all else. The 3.0-amp motor maintains pad speed under load — cheap sanders slow down when you press them into the work, which creates uneven scratch patterns. The through-the-pad dust collection pulls debris through holes in the sanding disc rather than around the edge of the pad, improving both visibility and finish quality. The lowest speed setting (4,000 OPM) is slower than most sanders go, which matters when you're sanding between coats of finish and don't want to burn through.

The tradeoff is weight. At 4.2 lbs, the Makita is noticeably heavier than the Bosch. For vertical surfaces or overhead work, the extra pound matters. For horizontal surfaces on a workbench, the weight helps the sander do the work — you guide it rather than press it.

Best Budget: WEN 6301

The WEN 6301 costs $25. It sands. It has a 5-inch pad. It spins. That's the feature list. The 1.0-amp motor is underpowered, the dust bag is mostly decorative, and vibration is higher than the Bosch or Makita. But if you refinish one piece of furniture a year or need a dedicated sander for between-coat finish work that you don't want to contaminate your main sander's pad with, $25 is hard to argue with.

Best Cordless: DeWalt DCW210B

Sanders don't need the raw power of a circular saw, which makes them good candidates for cordless use. The DeWalt DCW210B runs on the 20V MAX platform and delivers runtime comparable to a corded sander's convenience — roughly 35 minutes of continuous sanding on a 5Ah battery. The brushless motor means no maintenance and more efficient power delivery. If you work outdoors, on scaffolding, or anywhere an extension cord is a tripping hazard, the cordless premium is worth it.

Random Orbital vs Sheet Sander vs Belt Sander

  • Random orbital (this article): The all-rounder. The pad spins AND oscillates in a random pattern, so no two passes follow the same path. This prevents swirl marks. Use for: general smoothing, finish preparation, between-coat sanding. Start here.
  • Sheet sander (1/4-sheet or 1/2-sheet): Vibrates in a straight line. Slower material removal. Leaves a linear scratch pattern that's more visible. Use for: corners, edges, and when you need a rectangular pad shape. Mostly obsolete for general use.
  • Belt sander: A continuous sanding belt running at high speed. Removes material aggressively — think power planer with sandpaper. Use for: heavy stock removal, flattening slabs, stripping old paint. NOT for finish work. NOT your first sander.

Grit Guide: What Sandpaper to Use When

GritUse
40–60Aggressive stock removal, stripping old finish or paint
80–100Initial smoothing after planing or rough cutting
120–150Final smoothing before finish. The sweet spot for most wood
180–220Between-coat sanding of varnish, polyurethane, or paint
320+Final finish polishing. Wet sanding between final topcoats

The golden rule: Don't skip grits. Going from 80 to 220 means you'll spend twice as long sanding out 80-grit scratches that 120-grit would have removed in half the time. Standard progression: 80 → 120 → 180 → 220.

The Bottom Line

The Bosch ROS20VSC at $69 is the sander most people should buy. Low vibration, good dust collection, variable speed, proven reliability. Woodworkers building furniture: step up to the Makita BO5041K for the finer speed control and better dust collection. On a shoestring budget: WEN 6301 at $25 sands — sometimes that's enough. And buy good sandpaper. Diablo's 50-pack hook-and-loop assortment ($15) is the sweet spot between quality and price.
